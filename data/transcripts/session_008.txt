TRANSCRIPT

Therapist: How have you been since last session? We did some intense imaginal exposure work and I want to check in on how you processed that.

Client: It was hard. The recording — I listened to it twice like you said. The first time I kind of dissociated and couldn't really stay present. The second time was better. I cried through most of it but I actually stayed with the memory.

Therapist: Staying with the memory instead of dissociating is significant progress. The dissociation is your brain's way of protecting you from the pain, but it also prevents processing. The fact that you could stay present the second time tells me the processing is working. What was your SUDS level when you started listening versus at the end?

Client: Started at maybe 80. By the end it was around 60. Last week in session it peaked at 90 so that's lower.

Therapist: That decrease is exactly what we want to see. With repeated exposure, the emotional charge decreases. It's called habituation. The memory doesn't change, but your nervous system's response to it changes. Did anything new come up during the listening?

Client: Yeah, actually. I remembered something I hadn't talked about before. After the explosion, when I was trying to get to Ramirez — I could hear him calling for help. And I remember freezing for a few seconds before I moved. Those few seconds... I've always thought if I hadn't frozen, I could have saved him.

Therapist: That's a significant piece of the trauma that we haven't processed yet. The thought is "If I hadn't frozen, I could have saved him." Let's look at that. In the moments after an explosion, your body's freeze response is an involuntary neurological reaction. It's not a choice.

Client: But other guys moved right away. Jenkins was already moving toward the vehicle before I could get my legs to work.

Therapist: Different people have different nervous system responses, and those responses can vary even within the same person depending on proximity to the blast, prior exposure, and a dozen other factors. You did move. You did get to Ramirez. How long was the freeze?

Client: Maybe three or four seconds. But it felt like an hour.

Therapist: Three to four seconds. And the medical examiner's report — do you remember what it said about Ramirez's injuries?

Client: That the injuries were... that they were not survivable. From the blast itself.

Therapist: So the injuries that caused his death happened in the moment of the explosion — before the freeze, before you moved, before Jenkins moved. Those three to four seconds did not determine the outcome.

Client: I know that intellectually. The VA therapist told me the same thing. But I still feel like I let him down.

Therapist: The guilt you carry is what we call survivor guilt, and it serves a function similar to self-blame in other trauma — it gives your mind a sense that there was something you could have done, which feels more tolerable than the helplessness of knowing there was nothing anyone could do. The truth — that it was random, that you survived and he didn't through no fault of your own — is actually harder to accept than the guilt.

Client: That's... yeah. The randomness is the worst part. Why him and not me? We were standing three feet apart.

Therapist: And that question may never have a satisfying answer. Part of the work of trauma recovery is learning to live with that unanswerable question without it consuming your present. Speaking of the present — how are things with your wife and daughter?

Client: Better, actually. I took my daughter to the park this weekend. I was hypervigilant the whole time — scanning the perimeter, jumped when a kid popped a balloon. But I stayed. I didn't leave.

Therapist: That's a win. You tolerated the discomfort and stayed present with your daughter. How was it for her?

Client: She was so happy. She kept saying "Daddy, come push me on the swings." And I did.

Therapist: Hold onto that. That moment — choosing to stay despite the anxiety — is what recovery in action looks like. How is the drinking?

Client: Down to two beers a night. No hard liquor in three weeks. The urge is still there but I'm using the exercise instead — did thirty pushups last night when I wanted to pour a whiskey.

Therapist: That's excellent. The physical outlet for the anxiety is a much healthier pattern. I'd like to continue the imaginal exposure next session, specifically focusing on the freeze memory and the guilt about Ramirez. Are you ready for that?

Client: Yeah. It's hard, but I can feel something shifting. Like the weight is getting a little lighter.

Therapist: That's the processing working. Any thoughts of self-harm or suicide this week?

Client: No. I've got too much to live for now.

Therapist: Good. Keep listening to the recording, and I'll see you next week.
