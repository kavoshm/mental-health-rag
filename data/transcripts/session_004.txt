Therapist: Good afternoon. You mentioned in your message that things escalated this weekend. What happened?

Client: We had another blowup. Saturday night, we were supposed to have dinner together — I cooked, set the table, the whole thing. He came home two hours late from golfing and didn't even call. When I said something about it, he got defensive and said I was being controlling.

Therapist: How did you respond?

Client: Not well. I yelled. I said "Maybe if you cared about this marriage as much as your golf buddies, we wouldn't need therapy." Then he walked out and slept in the guest room. We haven't really spoken since.

Therapist: I can hear how hurtful that felt — the effort you put in and the feeling that it wasn't valued. Let's slow this down and look at what happened underneath the anger. What were you feeling before the yelling?

Therapist: So the dinner was an attempt at connection, and when he was late without calling, it felt like rejection of that attempt.

Client: Exactly. And it's not just the dinner. It's a pattern. I plan things, he cancels or shows up late. I suggest date nights, he says he's too tired. I feel like I'm in this marriage alone.

Therapist: That loneliness within a relationship is one of the most painful experiences. Have you been able to express that feeling — the loneliness — to Marcus directly, without it escalating?

Client: No, because every time I try to bring something up, it becomes a fight. He says I'm nagging or being dramatic. So I either hold it in until I explode, or I just give up.

Therapist: That pattern — holding it in, building resentment, then explosive release — is really common, and it makes it very hard for either person to actually hear the other. We've been working on "I statements" and emotional naming. Were you able to use any of those Saturday?

Client: I tried at first. I said "I feel hurt when plans we make aren't kept." But he responded with "I was only two hours late, it's not a big deal." And then I lost it.

Therapist: That's a core wound — feeling like your emotions aren't worthy of attention. Does that resonate with experiences earlier in your life?

Therapist: Your feelings are entirely valid. The intensity of the reaction might be amplified by the older wound, which is what we can work on. Just that awareness can help moderate the intensity.
