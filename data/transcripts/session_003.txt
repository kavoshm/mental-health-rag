TRANSCRIPT

Therapist: How are you doing this week?

Client: Not great. Saturday would have been our thirtieth wedding anniversary. I thought I was ready for it, but I spent most of the day in bed crying. My daughter came over and tried to get me to go out for lunch, but I couldn't do it.

Therapist: Anniversary dates are incredibly painful in the first year after a loss. How long has it been now since Robert passed?

Client: Seven months. Seven months and it still feels like yesterday. Sometimes I wake up and forget for a second, and then I remember and it hits me all over again.

Therapist: That morning moment of re-realization is one of the most painful experiences of grief. It's very common, especially in the first year. What does the rest of a typical day look like for you right now?

Client: I get up around nine. Robert used to make coffee every morning, so I just have tea now — I can't bring myself to use the coffee maker. I'll watch some TV, maybe do a little laundry. My daughter calls around noon. Afternoons are the hardest. That's when Robert and I would go for walks or work in the garden. I just sit in the living room and look at the garden going to pieces.

Therapist: You mentioned the garden. That was something meaningful you shared with Robert?

Client: It was his pride and joy. We spent twenty years building it together. Now the weeds are taking over and I can't... I can't go out there. It's too much.

Therapist: It sounds like the garden holds a lot of meaning and a lot of pain simultaneously. I wonder if, when you're ready, it might also be a place of connection with Robert. But there's no rush.

Client: My grief group leader said something similar. She said some people find comfort in continuing shared activities, but only when it feels right, not forced.

Therapist: That's good advice. Tell me about the grief group — how has that been going?

Client: It's been helpful actually. There's a woman named Dorothy whose husband died of pancreatic cancer, same as Robert. We've become friends. We talk on the phone sometimes. She's about a year ahead of me and she says it does get more bearable, though never really better.

Therapist: Having someone who truly understands your experience is invaluable. I'm glad you've made that connection. I want to check in on something — last session you mentioned feeling guilty when you have moments of laughter or enjoyment. Has that come up this week?

Client: Yes. Dorothy and I were talking on the phone and she said something funny and I laughed — really laughed — for the first time in months. And then immediately I felt this wave of guilt, like how dare I laugh when Robert is gone.

Therapist: Let's explore that guilt. If the roles were reversed — if you had passed and Robert was here — would you want him to never laugh again?

Client: No, of course not. I'd want him to find happiness again.

Therapist: What do you think Robert would say to you about that laughter on the phone?

Client: He'd probably say "About time, Marge." He had that kind of humor. He'd want me to laugh.

Therapist: That knowing — that Robert would want you to experience joy — is important. The guilt you feel isn't a reflection of disloyalty. It's actually a reflection of how much you loved him. But it can become a trap that keeps you frozen.

Client: I know that in my head. It's getting my heart to believe it that's the hard part.

Therapist: That's the work of grief — bridging that gap between what you know intellectually and what you feel emotionally. It takes time, and it takes moments like the one with Dorothy where joy breaks through despite the pain.

Client: I do want to get to a place where I can remember him and smile instead of just cry.

Therapist: That's a beautiful goal, and I believe you'll get there. Before we wrap up — any thoughts of not wanting to go on, or feeling like life isn't worth living?

Client: No, nothing like that. I'm sad, deeply sad, but I have my daughter and my grandson and I want to be here for them.

Therapist: That's good to hear. For this week, I'd like you to try one small thing: pick up one of the photo albums you and Robert made and look through it for just five minutes. If it feels too much, you can stop. But see if you can hold both the sadness and the gratitude at the same time.

Client: I'll try. That feels manageable.
