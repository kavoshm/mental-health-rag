Therapist: Hey, good to see you. How's the week been?

Client: It's whatever. Same stuff.

Therapist: Sounds like you're not feeling great about it. Want to tell me more, or would you rather start somewhere else?

Client: I got in a fight with my mom again. She went through my phone and found messages between me and Tyler. She freaked out.

Therapist: What was she upset about in the messages?

Client: Tyler and I were venting about school and I said something like "I literally want to disappear." She took it as a suicide threat and was crying and yelling and wanted to take me to the ER. It was so embarrassing.

Therapist: I can understand that felt embarrassing. Can I ask though — when you wrote "I want to disappear," what did you mean by it?

Client: Just that I'm over it. School sucks, everyone is fake, and I'm tired of pretending to be fine. I didn't mean I want to kill myself or whatever.

Therapist: Okay, and I believe you. Sometimes those words carry a lot of weight for parents, especially given your history. Your mom is scared because she loves you. Does that make sense even though it felt like an overreaction?

Client: I guess. But she doesn't trust me at all. Going through my phone is such an invasion of privacy.

Therapist: Let's hold both things at once — your right to some privacy, and her concern as a parent who's trying to keep you safe. Is there a middle ground you two could agree on?

Client: I don't know. She wants to read every message and I want her to stay out of it completely. There's no middle ground.

Therapist: What if the middle ground wasn't about the phone specifically, but about giving your mom enough reassurance that she doesn't feel she needs to check? Like, what if you had a daily check-in with her where you actually tell her how you're doing, and in exchange she agrees not to go through your messages?

Client: That might work. But I'm bad at talking about feelings. She asks "how was your day" and I just say "fine."

Therapist: And then she doesn't get the information she needs, so she goes looking for it elsewhere. You're both stuck in a cycle. Would you be willing to try a five-minute check-in using the mood scale we talked about? Just a number from one to ten, and one sentence about your day. That's it. Short enough that it doesn't feel like an interrogation.

Client: I could probably do a number.

Therapist: Let's try it for this week. Now I want to check in about the cutting. Your mom told me you were three weeks clean. Is that accurate?

Client: Yeah, three weeks. I've been using the ice cube thing instead. When I feel the urge, I hold ice in my hand. It hurts enough to kind of... reset my brain without actually hurting myself.

Therapist: That's a huge step. You're finding a way to get the same emotional relief without the self-harm. How many times have you used the ice this week?

Client: Twice. Once after the fight with my mom, and once on Thursday when I failed a math test.

Therapist: So the triggers are still there — conflict with mom, academic pressure — but you're responding differently. That's what recovery looks like. How does it feel to be three weeks without cutting?

Client: Honestly? A little proud. But also kind of like I'm waiting for it to not work anymore.

Therapist: That fear is normal. What would help if you have a moment where the ice isn't enough?

Client: I could text the crisis line? Or call you?

Therapist: Both are good options. Let me add — if you're ever in a moment where you feel like you might hurt yourself seriously, you can also tell your mom. I know that feels hard, but she wants to help even if the way she shows it sometimes feels like too much.

Client: Maybe. Can we talk about something else? How do I get people at school to stop being so fake?

Therapist: We can definitely talk about that. Tell me what's been happening.

Client: There's this group of girls who pretended to be my friends and then started a group chat without me and were talking about me. One of them sent me a screenshot to be mean. It said I was "weird" and "trying too hard."

Therapist: That's cruel, and I'm sorry that happened. How did it make you feel?

Client: Like there's something wrong with me. Like no matter what I do, I'll always be the weird one.

Therapist: The thought "there's something wrong with me" — does that feel familiar? Like a thought that shows up a lot?

Client: Yeah, it's kind of always there.

Therapist: That's a core belief we've been working on. The truth is, those girls' cruelty says more about them than about you. But I know that doesn't make it hurt less. For this week, in addition to the check-ins with mom and using ice instead of cutting, I'd like you to write down three things about yourself that Tyler — someone who actually knows you — would say are good. Can you do that?
