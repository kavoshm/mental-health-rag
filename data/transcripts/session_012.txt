TRANSCRIPT

Therapist: How has your mood been this week? We've been monitoring closely since the medication adjustment.

Client: It's been a weird week. I think I might be heading into a hypomanic episode. I've been sleeping less — about four hours a night — but I feel amazing. I reorganized my entire apartment at 3 AM on Tuesday. I started two new business ideas and bought three domain names. I signed up for a half marathon even though I haven't run in a year.

Therapist: I appreciate you recognizing these patterns. Let's go through our checklist. Decreased need for sleep — yes. Increased goal-directed activity — the apartment, the business ideas, the marathon. Any increase in spending?

Client: I bought a new laptop for one of the business ideas. Nine hundred dollars. And I signed up for an online course. Another two hundred.

Therapist: Is that within your normal budget?

Client: Not really. But in the moment it felt completely justified. Like, I need the laptop for the business, the business will make money, so it's an investment. My sister told me I was being manic and I got really irritated with her.

Therapist: The irritability when someone points it out — that's also a pattern. How are your thoughts? Are they racing?

Client: Yes, actually. I have a million ideas and they're all competing for attention. I started writing a novel at 2 AM last night. I haven't written fiction since high school but it felt like the ideas were just pouring out.

Therapist: On a scale of 1 to 10, where 1 is your depressive baseline and 10 is a full manic episode, where would you put yourself right now?

Client: Maybe a 6? Last time I had a full manic episode I maxed out two credit cards and drove to Las Vegas on a Tuesday. I'm not there. But I can feel the energy climbing.

Therapist: The fact that you can rate yourself and compare to previous episodes shows good insight. That self-awareness is one of your strengths in managing this condition. Let me ask about the medication — you mentioned the adjustment last session. Are you taking everything as prescribed?

Client: I'm on lithium 900mg at bedtime and lamotrigine 200mg. I've been taking them. But I'll be honest — part of me doesn't want to take them because I feel so good right now. The depression was horrible for three months and now I finally feel alive and I don't want it to stop.

Therapist: I completely understand that. The hypomania can feel like relief after a depressive episode, and the temptation to ride it is very real. But based on your history, what happens after the hypomanic phase?

Client: I crash. Hard. Last time I went from hypomania to the worst depression of my life in about two weeks. I couldn't get out of bed for a month.

Therapist: So the pattern is: depression, then hypomania that feels like freedom, then a crash into worse depression. The medication doesn't take away your creativity or energy — it smooths the cycle so you don't get the devastating crashes. Does that framework help?

Client: Yeah, when you put it that way. I just miss feeling... like myself. Sometimes I don't know which version is the real me — the depressed one, the hypomanic one, or the medicated one.

Therapist: That identity question is one of the most profound challenges of bipolar disorder. The real you is not defined by any mood state — it's the person who sits here, reflecting on their experience with honesty and trying to make good decisions. That person exists in every phase.

Client: I like that way of thinking about it.

Therapist: Let's talk about practical steps for right now. First, medication adherence is critical — please do not skip or reduce doses. The lithium is what prevents the escalation. Second, I want you to activate your sleep protocol — even if you don't feel tired, get in bed by 11 PM, no screens, use the relaxation exercises. Sleep disruption fuels hypomania.

Client: What about the business ideas and the marathon?

Therapist: I'd like you to apply the 48-hour rule we discussed. No financial decisions over one hundred dollars and no major commitments until you've waited 48 hours and run the idea by a trusted person — your sister, your sponsor from the support group, or me. If it still seems like a good idea after 48 hours and a reality check, it might be genuinely good. If the urgency has faded, it was the hypomania talking.

Client: That's fair. I can do the 48-hour rule. I'll call my sister about the laptop — maybe I should return it.

Therapist: That would be a wise decision to consider. I'm also going to contact your psychiatrist today to discuss whether a temporary lithium adjustment is needed. Is that okay?

Client: Yes, definitely.

Therapist: Any thoughts of self-harm? Sometimes in mixed states there can be impulsive thoughts.

Client: No, nothing like that. I feel too good right now, which is the problem.

Therapist: Right. And your awareness that feeling too good is a warning sign — that's the insight that protects you. Let's schedule a check-in call for Wednesday in addition to our regular session next week. I want to monitor this closely.

Client: Okay. Thank you for taking it seriously even though I feel fine. I know that's the whole point.

Therapist: Exactly. That's the wisdom of experience. I'll talk to you Wednesday.
