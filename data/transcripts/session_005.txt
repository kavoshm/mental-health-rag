TRANSCRIPT

Therapist: Good to see you. How has the week been?

Client: Rough. I relapsed on Wednesday. I had twenty-three days clean and then I used.

Therapist: I appreciate you telling me that directly. Can you walk me through what happened?

Client: I got a call from my ex. She said she's filing for full custody of our daughter. She said I'm an unfit parent because of the drug use. I just... everything fell apart. I called my old buddy and he came over with some stuff and that was it.

Therapist: What was the substance?

Client: Methamphetamine. I smoked it. Used for about six hours that night. Then I crashed and slept for almost two days.

Therapist: Let me ask about quantity and any current use — have you used since Wednesday?

Client: No. Wednesday was the only time. I woke up Friday feeling disgusted with myself. I called my sponsor and went to a meeting Friday night.

Therapist: Reaching out to your sponsor and going to a meeting after a relapse — that's a recovery behavior. Even in the relapse, you demonstrated something important: you didn't let it become a week-long binge. How are you feeling physically right now?

Client: Tired. A little down. But the acute stuff has passed. I'm eating and sleeping okay now.

Therapist: Good. Let's talk about the trigger. The phone call from your ex — what emotions came up in that moment?

Client: Fear. Pure fear. The thought of losing my daughter is the scariest thing in the world. And then shame — because she's right, I do have a problem. And then anger — at myself, at her, at the whole situation. And I didn't know what to do with all of that.

Therapist: So you had a massive emotional surge — fear, shame, anger — and the coping mechanism that's been wired in for years activated. The meth provided immediate relief from those feelings.

Client: Yeah, for about six hours I didn't feel any of it. And then it all came back ten times worse.

Therapist: That's the cycle, right? The temporary relief followed by amplified pain plus the added guilt of using. Let's look at your relapse prevention plan. What alternative coping strategies did we identify?

Client: Call my sponsor. Go to a meeting. Do the breathing exercises. Go for a run. And there was one more... the HALT thing — check if I'm Hungry, Angry, Lonely, or Tired.

Therapist: Right. And in that moment after the phone call, which of those did you try before using?

Client: None of them. It happened so fast. From phone call to calling my buddy was maybe ten minutes. I didn't even think about the plan.

Therapist: That's important information. The emotional intensity was so high that it bypassed your rational planning. This tells us we need to work on two things: building in a pause mechanism before action, and processing the custody situation so it's not as emotionally overwhelming.

Client: I just keep thinking — if I lose my daughter, what's the point of staying clean?

Therapist: I hear how central she is to your recovery motivation. And I want to gently challenge that thought — because if the only reason you stay clean is for her, then every custody setback becomes a relapse trigger. We need to find motivation that includes her but doesn't depend entirely on her. What would staying clean mean for you, separate from the custody situation?

Client: I guess... being someone I can look in the mirror and respect. Being the dad she deserves whether I have custody or not. Being alive in ten years instead of dead from an overdose.

Therapist: Those are powerful reasons. Let's hold onto those. Regarding the custody situation — have you spoken with your attorney?

Client: I have a legal aid lawyer. She said my completing treatment is the strongest thing I can do for my case. Every clean drug test helps.

Therapist: She's right. And Wednesday's relapse — have you disclosed that?

Client: Not yet. I'm afraid it'll hurt my case.

Therapist: I understand that fear. But honesty is generally the stronger position, especially if you can show that you responded to the relapse with appropriate recovery behaviors. Talk to your lawyer about how to handle it. Let's plan for next week — I want to update your relapse prevention plan with a specific "pause protocol" for when you get emotionally triggered. Five minutes between trigger and action. Can you commit to that?

Client: Five minutes. I can try.

Therapist: Are you having any thoughts of self-harm or suicide?

Client: No. I'm discouraged but not hopeless. I have my daughter to fight for.

Therapist: Good. Keep going to meetings, stay in contact with your sponsor, and I'll see you next week.
