TRANSCRIPT

Therapist: Last session we started working on your stuck points related to the assault. You wrote the impact statement I assigned. Would you like to read it?

Client: I'll try. I wrote it on Wednesday and I've read it to myself a few times but it's still hard. Okay. "Before the assault, I believed the world was generally safe and that I could protect myself. I believed that I was a strong person who could handle anything. The night of November 15th changed everything. A man I trusted, someone I considered a friend, assaulted me in my own apartment after I invited him over for dinner. Since that night, I believe that I cannot trust my own judgment. I believed he was safe and I was wrong. If I was wrong about him, I could be wrong about anyone. I no longer feel safe in my own home. I check the locks five or six times before bed. I cannot be alone with any man, even my brother, without my heart racing. I feel dirty and broken. I believe the assault was my fault because I invited him in."

Therapist: Thank you for sharing that. I can see how much courage it took to write and read those words. I want to sit with what you've said for a moment before we start examining it... How are you feeling right now?

Client: Shaky. My chest is tight. But not as bad as I expected, actually.

Therapist: You're doing important work. Let me reflect back some of the stuck points I heard in your statement. The first is "I cannot trust my own judgment." The second is "I am dirty and broken." The third is "The assault was my fault because I invited him in." Do those sound right?

Client: Yes. Those are the thoughts that go through my head every day.

Therapist: Let's start with the last one, because it's often the most impactful. You said the assault was your fault because you invited him into your apartment. Is inviting a friend over for dinner an unusual or dangerous activity?

Client: No. People do it all the time.

Therapist: When you invited him, were you inviting him to assault you?

Client: No, of course not. I was inviting him for dinner. We'd been friends for two years.

Therapist: So the invitation was for dinner, not for what happened. Whose decision was it to commit the assault?

Client: His.

Therapist: And could you have predicted that he would do this based on your prior experience with him?

Client: No. He'd never done anything like that before. He was always respectful. Everyone liked him.

Therapist: So you made a reasonable decision based on the information you had — inviting a trusted friend for a normal social activity. He made the criminal decision. Where does the responsibility actually lie?

Client: With him. When you say it like that, it's clear. But I still feel responsible somehow.

Therapist: That feeling is very common among survivors, and it serves a psychological function. If you can find a way it was your fault, then you can also believe you can prevent it from happening again — it gives you a sense of control. But that sense of control comes at the cost of carrying blame that isn't yours.

Client: I never thought about it that way. So the self-blame is actually trying to protect me?

Therapist: In a way, yes. It's your mind's attempt to regain a sense of safety. But there are healthier ways to rebuild that safety — ones that don't require you to carry the weight of someone else's crime. Let's look at the second stuck point: "I am dirty and broken." What evidence supports that belief?

Client: I don't have evidence, it's just... a feeling. Like something permanent changed inside me.

Therapist: Something did change — your sense of safety was violated, and that's a real injury. But does an injury make you broken, or does it make you injured? Because injuries heal.

Client: Injured... I guess injured is different from broken. Broken sounds permanent.

Therapist: That's an important distinction. You're not broken. You're injured, and you're actively doing the hardest work of healing by being here and processing this. For next week, I'd like you to write an alternative version of your impact statement — same events, but challenge the stuck points we discussed today. Write what you now understand, even if it doesn't fully feel true yet.

Client: Okay. That feels scary but doable.

Therapist: One more check-in — any thoughts of self-harm? How are you sleeping?

Client: No self-harm thoughts. Sleep is still rough — I wake up from nightmares about once a week, down from almost nightly. The prazosin is helping.

Therapist: Good. The decrease in nightmares is a positive sign that the processing work is helping. I'll see you next week.
