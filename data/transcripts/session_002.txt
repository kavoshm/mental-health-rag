TRANSCRIPT

Therapist: Welcome back. Last week we started working on identifying your automatic thoughts during panic attacks. How did the thought journal go?

Client: I actually filled it out three times this week. Two of them were during actual panic attacks, and one was when I felt the anxiety building but it didn't become full-blown.

Therapist: That's excellent — catching it in the building phase is exactly what we're working toward. Let's look at the entries. Can you walk me through the first one?

Client: Tuesday morning. I was driving to work and got stuck in traffic on the highway. My heart started pounding and I got this wave of dizziness. The thought was "I'm having a heart attack, I'm going to die right here in this car."

Therapist: And what was the emotion and intensity?

Client: Terror. Like a 9 out of 10. I pulled over to the shoulder and called my husband crying.

Therapist: Okay. Now, looking back at it, what evidence do you have that it was a heart attack versus a panic attack?

Client: Well... I've been to the ER twice and they did EKGs and blood work both times and everything was normal. My cardiologist cleared me. So logically I know my heart is fine. But in the moment, it just feels so real.

Therapist: That's the key tension we're working with. Your body produces real, intense physical sensations, and your threat system interprets them as danger. The sensations are real — the interpretation is what we can change. What alternative thought did you come up with?

Client: "This is a panic attack. It feels terrible but it is not dangerous and it will pass within 20 minutes."

Therapist: And how believable was that alternative thought in the moment?

Client: Maybe 30 percent. But later, after it passed, more like 80 percent.

Therapist: That gap is normal and it closes with practice. What about the third entry — the one where you caught it early?

Client: That was Thursday. I was in the grocery store and started feeling lightheaded. I noticed my breathing was getting shallow. Instead of the catastrophic thought, I caught myself and said "my body is reacting to stress, I need to slow my breathing." I did the diaphragmatic breathing right there in the cereal aisle and it actually worked. The feeling passed in about five minutes.

Therapist: That is a really significant moment. You interrupted the panic cycle before it escalated. How did that feel?

Client: Honestly, kind of empowering? Like maybe I'm not completely at the mercy of this thing.

Therapist: You're not. And what you just described is the goal of CBT — catching the automatic thought, challenging it, and replacing it with a more accurate one. The more you practice, the more automatic the alternative thought becomes.

Client: I just wish I could do it every time.

Therapist: Progress isn't linear. You'll have good days and harder days. The fact that you did it once means the neural pathway exists. Now we strengthen it. For this week, I'd like you to continue the thought journal and add one new element: rate how believable the alternative thought is at the time and then again one hour later. That tracking will show you the gap closing over time.

Client: Okay, I can do that.

Therapist: How are the panic attacks in terms of frequency? You said they were daily when we started.

Client: Down to maybe three or four a week now. Still too many, but better than seven.

Therapist: That's meaningful improvement in just four sessions. Let's also talk about the avoidance — are you still avoiding the highway?

Client: Yes. I've been taking back roads to work. It adds twenty minutes.

Therapist: That avoidance is actually maintaining the anxiety. Next week, I'd like to start planning a gradual exposure hierarchy for driving situations. We'll start very small and work up. Are you willing to try that?

Client: It scares me, but I trust the process. Let's do it.
