TRANSCRIPT

Therapist: This is our twelfth session, and I wanted to take some time today to review your progress. How are you feeling about where you are compared to when we started?

Client: It's kind of night and day, honestly. When I came in here three months ago, I was having panic attacks every single day. I couldn't drive on the highway. I was afraid to go to the grocery store alone. I thought I was having heart attacks constantly.

Therapist: And now?

Client: Last week I had zero panic attacks. Zero. I drove on the highway to visit my sister — that's a forty-minute drive. I went to the grocery store on Saturday and even though I felt a little anxious in the checkout line, I used the breathing technique and it passed in about two minutes.

Therapist: That's remarkable progress. Let's put some numbers to it. Your initial GAD-7 was 19 — severe anxiety. Today's score is 7 — mild. Your panic frequency went from daily to maybe once every week or two. And you've eliminated most of your avoidance behaviors. How does it feel to hear those numbers?

Client: It feels good. I mean, I still have anxiety. I don't think that's ever going to completely go away. But I feel like I have tools now. Like I'm not helpless against it anymore.

Therapist: That's exactly the right mindset. The goal was never to eliminate anxiety — it's a normal human emotion. The goal was to change your relationship with it, so it doesn't control your life. Let's talk about the tools that have been most helpful.

Client: The thought challenging for sure. Being able to catch the catastrophic thought — "I'm dying, something is seriously wrong" — and replace it with "this is anxiety, it's uncomfortable but not dangerous, it will pass." That's been the biggest game-changer.

Therapist: What about the exposure work?

Client: That was terrifying but I think that's what actually broke through the avoidance. The hierarchy we built — starting with just sitting in the car on the highway without driving, then driving one exit, then two exits — each step was hard but I did it. And each time I survived, the fear got a little smaller.

Therapist: The interoceptive exposures — the spinning, the hyperventilation exercises — were those helpful?

Client: Yes, actually. Once I understood that I could voluntarily create those physical sensations and nothing bad happened, it took away a lot of the fear of the sensations themselves. Like, dizziness doesn't mean I'm dying. It just means I'm dizzy.

Therapist: Exactly. You've essentially retrained your threat detection system. Now, I want to talk about maintenance and relapse prevention. Panic disorder can come back, especially during periods of high stress. What's your plan for handling a setback?

Client: First, I'd remind myself that a setback isn't the same as going back to square one. One panic attack doesn't mean I've lost everything. Second, I'd use the tools — breathing, thought challenging, and I'd increase my exercise because that's been really good for managing baseline anxiety. Third, I'd call you or schedule a booster session.

Therapist: That's a solid relapse prevention plan. I want to add one thing: watch for the return of avoidance behaviors. That's usually the first sign that anxiety is creeping back in. If you notice yourself taking back roads to avoid the highway, or skipping the grocery store, that's your early warning signal.

Client: Good point. The avoidance was so sneaky — it felt like a reasonable choice at the time even though it was making everything worse.

Therapist: That's the trap of avoidance — it reduces anxiety in the short term, which reinforces it, but it maintains and grows it in the long term. Let's discuss the plan going forward. I'm recommending we move to biweekly sessions for a month, then monthly check-ins, with the understanding that you can schedule sooner if needed. How does that sound?

Client: That sounds right. I feel ready to try more space between sessions. A month ago I would have panicked at the idea of not coming every week, which I think says something about my progress.

Therapist: It absolutely does. You're trusting yourself more, and that's earned. Any concerns about spacing out sessions?

Client: I guess just the fear of the unknown — like, what if something big triggers me and I can't handle it?

Therapist: That thought itself is worth challenging. What's the evidence that you can handle hard things now?

Client: Well, last week my husband's father had a medical emergency and I drove him to the hospital — on the highway, at night, in the rain — and I didn't panic. I was stressed, obviously, but I managed it. The old me couldn't have done that.

Therapist: That's your evidence. You showed up in a genuinely stressful situation and functioned. That's not just managing anxiety — that's resilience. I'm genuinely proud of the work you've done.

Client: Thank you. I couldn't have done it without this process. I'm really grateful.

Therapist: You did the hard part. I just provided the framework. Let's schedule our biweekly session and I'll see you in two weeks.
