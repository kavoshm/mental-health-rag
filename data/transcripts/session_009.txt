TRANSCRIPT

Therapist: Last week you told me about the job loss. How have things been since then?

Client: I've been applying to jobs online every day but haven't heard back from anyone. It's been two weeks since the layoff and the silence is crushing. I keep refreshing my email waiting for something.

Therapist: The waiting period after a layoff is incredibly stressful. How are you managing the day-to-day?

Client: Honestly, I've been pretending to go to work. I get dressed in the morning, leave the house, and go to the library or a coffee shop. My wife doesn't know. She thinks I still have the job.

Therapist: That's a significant piece of information. Can you tell me more about the decision to keep this from your wife?

Client: I'm supposed to be the provider. We just bought a house six months ago. We have a baby on the way — she's due in April. If she finds out I lost my job, she'll panic. Her whole family will judge me. I can't let that happen.

Therapist: I can hear the enormous pressure you're putting on yourself. You're carrying the financial stress, the job search stress, and the stress of maintaining a secret from the person closest to you. That's an incredible amount of weight for one person.

Client: I've been having chest pains. I went to urgent care and they said it was stress. My blood pressure is up too. The doctor wanted to start medication but I said no because I don't want my wife to see the pill bottle.

Therapist: The secrecy is now affecting your physical health. That's your body telling you this situation is unsustainable. I want to explore the belief underneath the secrecy: "I'm supposed to be the provider." Where does that belief come from?

Client: My father worked three jobs to support us. He never complained. He never asked for help. That's what a man does.

Therapist: And when your father modeled that, what did it cost him?

Client: He died at fifty-eight of a heart attack. He was stressed his whole life but never showed it.

Therapist: I notice something important in what you just said. Your father's model of never showing vulnerability, never asking for help, never admitting struggle — it literally killed him. And now you're developing stress-related chest pains and elevated blood pressure while following the same pattern. What do you make of that?

Client: When you put it that way... it's scary. I don't want to end up like him.

Therapist: That awareness is important. Let me ask this: if your wife lost her job, would you want her to hide it from you?

Client: No, absolutely not. I'd want to know so we could figure it out together.

Therapist: So you'd want to be a partner in solving the problem, but you're denying her the same opportunity. Is it possible that telling her, while terrifying in the short term, would actually reduce your overall stress by sharing the burden?

Client: Probably. But the thought of that conversation makes me want to throw up.

Therapist: That physical reaction tells me how much fear is tied to vulnerability for you. We can work on this. I'm not going to tell you to go home tonight and confess — the timing and approach matter. But I do want us to start planning for that conversation. What would you need to feel ready?

Client: I'd need to have a plan. Like, not just "I lost my job" but "I lost my job and here's what I'm doing about it and here's our financial runway."

Therapist: That's a reasonable approach. You're framing it as problem-solving rather than crisis-announcing. How long can you sustain the finances without her knowing?

Client: Realistically? Maybe another month. We have some savings and I got a small severance. But the mortgage, the baby costs coming up... it won't last long.

Therapist: So there's a timeline. Let's use our sessions to prepare you for that conversation. In the meantime, I want to address the physical symptoms — the chest pains and blood pressure. I strongly recommend you follow up with your primary care doctor and be honest with them. You don't have to tell your wife about the medication if it's prescribed, but ignoring cardiovascular symptoms is dangerous. Can you agree to that?

Client: Yeah, I can do that.

Therapist: Good. One more question — are you sleeping?

Client: Maybe four or five hours a night. I lie awake running numbers in my head. Calculating how long the money lasts.

Therapist: Sleep deprivation will make everything worse — your mood, your ability to interview well, your physical health. Let's add sleep hygiene to our work. I'll teach you a specific technique next session. For now — any thoughts of harming yourself?

Client: No, nothing like that. I'm stressed, not hopeless. I just need to find a job.

Therapist: Okay. For this week, I want you to write out the financial plan — actual numbers, runway, worst case scenario. Having it on paper instead of spinning in your head at 2 AM will help. And think about a timeline for telling your wife. We'll work on the how.

Client: Alright. Thanks, doc. This is helpful even though it's not what I wanted to hear.
